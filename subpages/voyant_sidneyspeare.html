VOYANT ANALYSIS

The following analysis employs Voyant tools to complement stylometric analysis performed via Normal Stylo and Craig’s Zeta. The tool allows for qualitative investigation thanks to visualizations of word frequencies, trends, and contextual tools such as collocations. The two tragedies in question are analyzed separately, to detect each author’s stylistic print, and then compared for the purpose of the project’s question.

Antony and Cleopatra is twice as long as Sidney’s, but The Tragedie of Antonie is twice as dense as Shakespeare’s, where lexical density is calculated as the ratio of unique words to total words in a text. It also has more average words per sentence. Comparing lexical density between shorter and longer texts is not particularly indicative: text length significantly impacts the measure, as longer texts naturally have more word repetition, lowering density. Also, some differences affect word choice and repetition: in fact, plays written to be performed like Antony and Cleopatra include dialogue and stage directions, while closet dramas like The Tragedie of Antonie don’t. The difference in readability could be attributed to the different styles and purposes of the two works. Sidney's translation was likely intended for a more limited, aristocratic audience, while Shakespeare's play was written for public performance.

The cirruses display groups of most frequent words encountered in the texts, expanding on the list contained in the panels. Stopwords like ‘the’, characters’ names and the verb ‘said’ were removed from the representation, in order to get more meaningful results. In fact, characters’ names took up a high percentage of Shakespeare’s cirrus. Sidney’s terms emphasize her archaic and formal literary style, more suited for the purpose of her piece, but also likely influenced by the desire to adhere to Garnier’s original.

The original visualizations featured a prominence of the verb ‘said’ and characters’ names in Antony and Cleopatra, once again suggesting its dialogue-based nature, which required more action and lines exchange. This shows the substantial difference of the word’s presence between the two tragedies, the left one being Antony and Cleopatra. This finding further discourages theories of stylistic similarities adopted by Shakespeare.

Sidney’s list features the pronoun “hir”, meaning “her”, suggesting a higher focus on Cleopatra’s figure, as the empirical evidence described it “distinctly female oriented” (Labbé, 2008, p. 2). In Sidney’s version, in fact, the action was much more focused on a couple of characters, namely Cleopatra and Antony, in contrast with Shakespeare who introduced many more. The word “loue”, archaic term for “love”, appears among them, reflecting its importance as a topic in the narration and the characters; it could also refer to other forms, such as loyalty or patriotism. Cleopatra is depicted by Sidney as more loyal to Antony than in the French counterpart, never mentioning any words that could suggest the adulterous nature of their relationship (she is never referred to as his “wife”, as Antony was still supposedly Octavia’s husband). The reason for this choice has to be researched in Sidney’s immense admiration towards Queen Elizabeth, famously and purposefully chaste. Cleopatra’s tragic demise as a queen is linked to her subjectivity to the power of love and passion, contrary to Elizabeth, whose integrity lead her to prosperously reign for many decades (Holz, 2022).

According to the most frequent collocations in Mary Sidney’s work, Cleopatra’s agency or empathy do not emerge distinctly, except for the link with the word ‘force’. Her name is manly linked to her figure as a mother and a partner (“children”, “Antonius”), prioritized over her duties as a queen. Other collocations feature the word “faire”, typical adjective of the classical tradition from which Sidney used to take inspiration, absent in William Shakespeare. Conversely, Shakespeare’s version presents collocations like “queen”, “peace”, “love”, and “pardon”, indicating a more well-defined character. One limitation has to be pointed out when analyzing Antony and Cleopatra: given the presence of characters’ names at the beginning of each line, some correlations are not detected correctly, as the correlated words already belong to the next character’s line.

Antony’s figure is mostly linked with either Cleopatra or feelings of sadness and subjugation (“soule”, “poore”, “woe”) or death, highlighting both tragedies’ emphasis on the emotional depth that the tragic story carries. Sidney’s version seems to particularly stress his suffering and vulnerability, while Shakespeare’s presents some more positive terms such as “good”, “great”, and “fight”, highlighting the strength of the character a bit more. Words like power, topical in the narration, have different representations across the tragedies, both in the correlations and in the overall usage, as exposed by the trends. In Antony and Cleopatra, the word carries contrasting, yet stronger connotations, even though the correlations are not specific and do not allow to see what characters the word is more linked to. In The Tragedie of Antonie, more timid words like “great” and “subdue” can be observed. As empirical evidence showed, Cleopatra’s personality is modified by Sidney to exert more power over Antony, which could be suggested by the verb “subdue”, although, as specified before, the context is not enough to tell.

Manually searching for alleged topical words involves a limitation per se, as it is highly likely to introduce bias. In fact, conducting term-based analysis inevitably involves a preselection on the researcher’s end according to his or her perception of significant or topical words. It forcefully narrows the scope of the investigation and potentially manipulates the results by focusing on specific topics. Trends of other topic words (“love”, “honor”, “duty”) were analyzed, in order to gain knowledge of their distribution across the scenes.

Antony and Cleopatra does not appear to contain the word “honor”, as it does not show up in the trends, suggesting a different type of take on morality. However, the term “love” appears to have a similar recurrence in the story. The parallel suggests that both authors recognized the centrality of love to the story of Antony and Cleopatra. Observing the collocations, however, unveils the different nuances in actually describing or portraying love in the story, starting from the significant difference in the number of times the word is present in the two tragedies.

“Duty”, on the other hand, appears more frequently in Shakespeare’s play rather than Sindey’s. The research in the phrases section, suggested by the analysis of Sidney’s translations, carried out separately, does not yield any significative results either. In fact, both stylistic patterns and constructions found similar in her two works, although not particularly significant, suggesting her translator’s signature style (“without his loue”, “you haue”), are not found in Shakespeare’s text. An exception is made for the expression “shall we”, which becomes “shall we do” in Sidney’s, but is common between the two.

The collocations, however, show differences in how the sentence is employed. Death is also a crucial term and topic in the two tragedies, both its relevance as a topic itself (all of Shakespeare’s characters die on stage), but most of all when connected with Cleopatra. In fact, her suicide scene has been speculated to be one of the most probable inspirations Shakespeare took from Sidney. In Sidney’s translation, death presents collocates such as “wounded”, “succour”, “inhumaine”, and “breath”, suggesting a focus on physical and emotional sufferings and describing it as a deeply human experience.

At the same time, other more positive collocations arise, such as “willing”, “sweetly”, and “noble”, possibly revealing Cleopatra’s approach to her suicide in a heroic way. In Antony and Cleopatra, the collocations suggest similar emotions, but conveyed with different words, such as “sorrow” or “suffer”, and “victorious” and “proud”.

In The Tragedie of Antonie, none of the collocations involve characters names, which instead appear in Shakespeare’s (“Diomedes”, “soldier”, “roman”), along with “scene”. This once more demonstrates the stylistic difference in portraying these types of scenes, never involving direct representation in Sidney. The correlations do not present any specific lexical similarities that could suggest borrowings. In both plays, Cleopatra's suicide is presented with elements of nobility and heroism. Sidney's use of "willing," "sweetly," and "noble," and Shakespeare's "victorious" and "proud" indicate a portrayal of Cleopatra's death as a triumphant act rather than a defeat, complying with Walker’s assumptions about similarities in exploring similar themes surrounding the queen’s death. Nevertheless, their approaches differ in terms of dramatic presentation and linguistic choices.


Conclusions

Based on the Voyant tools analysis complementing Stylo and Craig’s Zeta, the possibility of significant stylistic similarities or direct authorial attribution from Shakespeare towards Sidney appears limited. The quantitative disparities in sentence length, vocabulary density, and readability index reflect contrasting creative methodologies. Furthermore, the use of language once more highlights stylistic distinctions: Sidney’s work employs archaic and formal literary style, while Shakespeare’s play features more dialogue and action, as well as more modern style words, in order to engage the audience. Character portrayal is also distinct: while Cleopatra is allegedly expressed in a stronger way in The Tragedie of Antonie, collocations reflect her proximity with the role of mother and partner more than her role as a queen. Collocations with specific words also show distinct approaches: Shakespeare’s work presents stronger and more contrasting connotations, while Sidney’s uses more subdued language. Finally, the death scene presents similar connotations and themes, but differ in terms of language employed and portrayal. Shakespeare may have drawn inspiration from Mary Sidney’s The Tragedie of Antonie,, but adapting certain characteristics to suit his own dramatic style and the expectations of his audience. To investigate and substantiate the possibility of inspiration or influence, a close reading of both tragedies would be essential. The analysis could possibly unveil broader similarities in scene arrangement, thematic resonances or characterization. Studying how characters and themes are handled in context could highlight subtle borrowings or reinterpretations.

Limitations of the tool

As mentioned throughout the analysis, the manual selection of topical words for analysis introduces potential bias and reduces the scope of investigation. Moreover, character names at the start of lines in the works of Shakespeare may provide false detection of word correlations. Finally and most importantly, while Voyant tools are useful in visualizing word frequencies and trends, they cannot replace close reading and contextual analysis in comparing works of literature. Therefore, a definite answer to the question of authorial attribution and stylistic similarities cannot be given just by examining the two tragedies with these instruments. Close reading is required.

Future analysis

A further exploration could be carried out in order to investigate other possible influences Mary Sidney could have had on William Shakespeare. For instance, original works such as Sidney’s poems could be analyzed alongside Shakespeare’s sonnets, taking into consideration the limitations that analyzing poetry with stylo and voyant entail. Namely, figurative language that needs contextualization, challenges in comparing shorter and longer texts, difficulties with non-standard language, and, regarding Craig’s Zeta, the contained length which often does not allow for the analysis to be performed.
