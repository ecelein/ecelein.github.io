Additional Info:

  • Two branches can be seen. The top one, with a delta distance of 1.3, is composed of The Cantos by Ezra Pound. In this cluster, the main authors are Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot. In the most extreme node, it can be seen, with a distance of 0.8, The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock by T.S. Eliot and Hugh Selwyn Mauberley by Ezra Pound.

This overlap could indicate:

  • Both authors being active participants in the modernist movement, which may explain the similarities.
  • Their marital and literary collaboration leading to shared stylistic traits, especially in thematic or structural elements.
  • Ezra Pound serving as a valuable distractor. His works consistently form a separate cluster, demonstrating that the method Juola proposes effectively distinguishes between authors with distinct stylistic profiles.

The usage of 2-grams (word pairs) makes collocations and phrasal choices evident. Compared to MFW-100, it is more detailed, and the clusters are tighter due to the additional specificity word pairs bring.

Overlaps between T.S. Eliot and Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot persist, especially for texts like La Figlia che Piange, suggesting that phrase-level features alone may not fully separate authors of shared influences or collaborative histories. Further analysis using Voyant is recommended.

MFC 4-grams Culled @ 0% Classic Delta Distance

Character-level n-grams (4-grams in this case) focus on fine details such as punctuation and word endings. This approach captures stylistic elements not apparent at the word or phrase level:

  • Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot's texts cluster tightly due to her unique character-level stylistic patterns.
  • T.S. Eliot's texts cluster together, underlining his distinct stylistic features even at the micro-style level.
  • Ezra Pound's works remain distinct, reflecting his experimental use of language and form.

However, this approach might be more prone to noise, such as formatting differences or typographical errors, affecting clustering accuracy.

MFW 100 - 1000 @ 0% Classic Delta Distance Bootstrap Consensus Tree Consensus 0.5

The Bootstrap Consensus Tree provides a stability analysis of the relationships between texts based on repeated resampling of the datasets. The following stable clusters are observed:

  • Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot: Her texts (Bel Canto, Song in the Night, etc.) consistently form a stable cluster.
  • T.S. Eliot: His texts (The Waste Land, The Hollow Men) also cluster consistently.
  • Ezra Pound: His texts (The Cantos, Hugh Selwyn Mauberley) remain isolated, demonstrating his clear stylistic divergence.

However, the overlap between Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot and T.S. Eliot observed in the dendrograms is also evident in the bootstrap tree. Texts like La Figlia che Piange and The Coliseum Dome again show a closer proximity to T.S. Eliot’s works and must be investigated further.

DISTRACTOR AUTHORS

Ezra Pound

Ezra Pound was the editor and mentor of T.S. Eliot, helping Eliot shape his major work “The Waste Land”. Pound's influence upon modernist poetry was immense, not merely through editing but also through promoting most of the great literary figures of the time.

Poems:

  • Hugh Selwyn Mauberley
  • The Cantos
  • The Garden

Short Stories:

  • Literary Essays
  • Make it New Essays

Edna St. Vincent Millay

An American poet and playwright, Edna Millay wrote under the pseudonym of Nancy Boyd and won a Pulitzer Prize for Poetry for her poem “Ballad of the Harp-Weaver”. Edna Millay and T.S. Eliot were contemporaries in the early 20th century. Once in 1949, Millay planned a satire of T.S. Eliot, placing her awareness of their different worldviews.

Poetry:

  • Few Figs from Thistles
  • Renascence
  • Spring

Hilda Doolittle

Hilda Doolittle, who wrote under the pseudonym of H.D., was an American modernist poet, novelist, and memoirist. She and T.S. Eliot co-founded the avant-garde Imagist group of poets.

Poetry:

  • Eurydice
  • Heliodora and Other Poems
  • Sea Garden

Katherine Mansfield

A significant figure in the Modernist movement, Katherine Mansfield explored anxiety, sexuality, and existentialism in her writing. Both she and T.S. Eliot were central figures in modernist literature.

Short Story:

  • The Garden Party

Marianne Moore

An American modernist poet, critic, translator, and editor, Marianne Moore was nominated for the Nobel Prize. Eliot admired her work, considering her poetry “probably the most durable.” In 1935, Eliot, along with Ezra Pound, convinced Moore to allow Faber & Faber to publish her "Selected Poems," which Eliot himself edited, demonstrating his high regard for her work.

Poetry:

  • Poetry
  • The Fish
  • To a Snail

Thomas Hardy

An English novelist and poet, Thomas Hardy and T.S. Eliot were both influential figures in English literature. Eliot was notably critical of Hardy's work. In his 1933 lectures at the University of Virginia, later published as "After Strange Gods," Eliot harshly criticized Hardy's writing, accusing him of moral nihilism and describing his work as representing "the intrusion of the diabolic into modern literature."

Short Story:

  • A Tragedy of Two Ambitions

Virginia Woolf

An English writer and a major figure of the Modernist movement, Virginia Woolf and T.S. Eliot maintained a friendship for over twenty years. The two supported each other's work: Woolf set type for Eliot's publications and invited him for tea and weekends. Eliot also edited Moore's "Selected Poems" for Faber & Faber, a publishing house associated with both writers.

Short Story:

  • Kew Gardens

Wallace Stevens

Wallace Stevens thought of himself as the "dead opposite" to T.S. Eliot, emphasizing their different styles and orientations to poetry. Despite dealing with the same central modern theme—the human situation—they represented sharply differing approaches and outlooks.

Poetry:

  • The Idea of Order at Key West
  • The Snow Man
  • Thirteen Ways of Looking at a Blackbird

William Butler Yeats

W.B. Yeats and T.S. Eliot were two titans of 20th-century poetry. Their relationship was complicated, marked by mutual regard and professional contact alongside distinctly different poetic styles. Initially critical of Yeats, Eliot later revised his opinion, calling him "the greatest poet of his time."

Poetry:

  • Sailing to Byzantium
  • The Second Coming
  • The Tower

Short Story:

  • The Binding of the Hair
