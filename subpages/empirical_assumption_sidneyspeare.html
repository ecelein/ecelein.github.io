Research Question and Empirical Evidence

This project seeks to examine the distinctive writing styles and investigate the authorship of works by different clusters of authors: in this section, William Shakespeare and Mary Sidney will be the center of the analysis. The research is based on empirical assumptions about the two authors. The alleged influence of Mary Sidney on the Stratford-upon-Avon playwright’s works has sparked various debates among scholars, with particular scrutiny focused on The Tragedie of Antonie (1592) and Antony and Cleopatra (written between 1606-1607, published in the First Folio in 1623).

The temporal gap between the two works might corroborate the hypothesis, but is also a potentially compromising factor, as cultural and linguistic shifts may have occurred.

The Countess of Pembroke was one of the most educated women of her time, deeply immersed in the literary world as leader of the prestigious Wilton Circle, which fostered discussions among writers and intellectuals, possibly shaping the broader literary landscape in which Shakespeare operated. Considering the great challenges women authors faced in the Elizabethan era in publishing their original works, critics such as Winkler argue “Was Shakespeare’s name useful camouflage, allowing her to publish what she otherwise couldn’t?” (Winkler, 2019).

The hypothesis is corroborated by a further connection between the two authors, as Sidney and her husband, Henry Herbert, were patrons of Richard Burbage’s theatrical troupe, which performed plays by William Shakespeare in the 1590s. Furthermore, in “Quick Comedians”: Mary Sidney, Samuel Daniel and the Theatrum Mundi in Shakespeare’s Antony and Cleopatra”, Cadman investigates “clear intertextual links” between the plays “In spite of emerging from different dramatic traditions” (Cadman, 2015).

These connections are centered around how they portray Cleopatra and the concept of "theatrum mundi" or "the world as a stage" in politics, a concept born to portray and understand how rulers display and maintain their authority, as they are seen as actors performing a role for an audience.

All three plays show how Cleopatra is affected by the use of theatrical elements in politics. Specifically, in all three works, Cleopatra retreats to her tomb to commit suicide, using this private space to control how she will be remembered politically. She refuses to become a public spectacle in Rome, and carefully stages her death in a way that reflects the Roman ideal of choosing an honorable death over humiliation.

By highlighting these connections, we can see that Shakespeare's play isn't a complete break from earlier traditions. Instead, it continues and develops ideas already present in the works of Sidney and Daniel, particularly in how it portrays the relationship between theater and political power. Albeit concrete evidence remains elusive, these connections encourage more in-depth research to explore a potential influential relationship between the two authors.

Process Overview and Issues Encountered

As previously stated, the examination focuses on two specific works by the considered authors, as the empirical evidence consulted particularly highlighted connections between the two tragedies. Furthermore, an investigation across all genres and original works by Mary Sidney and William Shakespeare would have significantly widened the scope of the research, requesting a project of its own.

Considering The Tragedie of Antonie in opposition to Antony and Cleopatra immediately revealed a limitation to the authorial attribution query in a broad sense, as Mary Sidney’s work is a translation of French poet Robert Garnier’s Marc-Antoine (1578). Therefore, proving authorship attribution theories benefitting Mary Sidney presents its challenges: the derivative nature of the translation and linguistic differences inherently retain original structures and stylistic features that cannot be totally attributed to the translator, obscuring her unique authorial voice.

Nevertheless, her translation has been defined as “remarkably free” (Cerasano and Wynne-Davies in Labbé, 2008, p.4), starting from a change in the metric structure from alexandrine couplet to blank verse. Moreover, differences in the ways characters are portrayed, and some distinct nuances incorporated into their personalities have been pointed out. As Labbé argues, Sidney’s version is “distinctly female-oriented” (Labbé, 2008, p. 2), making Cleopatra a “more emphatic and determined heroine” (Labbé, 2008, p. 3) compared to the French counterpart.

Nonetheless, to better appreciate Mary Sidney’s signature alterations, and to confidently attribute any potential borrowings from Shakespeare to her, it would be necessary to compare the original French version of the work to her translation. The Voyant tools, though useful, fall short in carrying out an in-depth contextual analysis required for such comparison, which would require close reading, beyond the scope of the investigation.

In order to rectify this limitation, The Tragedie of Antonie was examined in relation to another one of Mary Sidney’s translations, A Discourse of Life and Death, via Voyant Tools. The test was carried out in an attempt to unveil any distinct patterns in the author’s style of translation to be later researched within the comparative analysis between her and Shakespeare’s tragedy.

Additionally to the previously mentioned limitation tied to Voyant tools, which cannot provide a satisfactory alternative to close reading, the two translations belong to different genres, constituting a source of confusion. In fact, The Tragedie of Antonie is, as the name suggests, a tragedy, while A Discourse of Life and Death is a philosophical treatise: it was not possible to avoid this discrepancy, as the author’s body of work did not include any other tragedies.

Albeit the similarity of the themes encountered in the two works, the comparison cannot be considered completely accurate, as the different genres inherently carry stylistic differences and distinct approaches in addressing the themes. Considering the limitations, the analysis was still performed, but it did not yield significant results. Some minor stylistic similarities and use of specific words were encountered; these features, however, did not emerge during the comparative study of both tragedies, corroborating the thesis of a lack of similarities between Shakespeare and Sidney’s works.

Following these considerations, the study’s focus shifted towards an exploration of stylistic parallels, beginning from Stylo analysis, following with Craig’s Zeta, and concluding with a comparison using Voyant.

Prior to the implementation of computational tools, an empirical investigation was conducted. This preliminary research highlighted substantial distinctions between the two tragedies, which proved fundamental in the subsequent interpretation of the outcomes.

Notably, the works exhibited fundamentally different dramatic natures: Sidney’s work was conceived as a closet drama, a literary piece meant to be recited within aristocratic circles, whereas Shakespeare’s tragedy was designed for public performance, intended to engage a diverse audience (Corporaal, 2003).

Consequently, the stylistic structures differ. The conversation in The Tragedie of Antonie is monopolized by the three main protagonists (Antony, Cleopatra, and Octavius) and predominantly rendered as monologue, while Antony and Cleopatra fosters dialogical action among a higher number of characters.

Furthermore, Sidney respects Garnier’s tradition by following the Aristotelian units of time, place, and action, and avoiding to portray deaths and fights, which usually happens off stage. The only exception is constituted by Cleopatra’s suicide. Shakespeare, by contrast, employs opposite strategies (Holz, 2022).
