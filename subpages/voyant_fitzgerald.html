Analysis of The Great Gatsby in Relation to F. Scott Fitzgerald's Life

  • Connections to Fitzgerald’s Personal Experiences: Gatsby's obsession with amassing wealth stems from his desire to win Daisy's love—a reflection of Fitzgerald's own struggles with social class and ambition, as he struggled to gain financial stability in order to gain a social status and marry Zelda.
  • Jazz Age Extravagance: Fitzgerald and Zelda lived extravagantly by hosting or attending lavish parties, but this lifestyle led to personal dissatisfaction, debt, and disappointment. Gatsby's tragic pursuit of happiness through material excess (endless parties every day, blockbuster production, with only the famous and wealthy invited), ultimately leaving him unfulfilled and isolated from the woman he loved.
  • Zelda vs. Daisy: Shared traits: beauty, charm, and a drive to acquire wealth and social status. About Zelda: Daisy's shallow, whimsical nature can be seen as Fitzgerald's critique of Zelda's materialistic tendencies. Just as Daisy retreats into her world of privilege, Zelda's mental health struggles and their turbulent relationship deeply affect Fitzgerald.
  • Women: The frequency of the term "women" in the text underscores the novel's focus on gender roles. Women like Daisy and Jordan Baker are presented as complex but often constrained by societal expectations. Is this a matter of tradition? Or are they simply the author's personal perceptions being reflected? Is the author actually challenging the patriarchal system? Fitzgerald's personal struggles with Zelda, who also challenged traditional roles, are about women resisting but remaining entangled in patriarchal systems.
  • Psychological and Emotional Nuances: Gatsby idealizes love as well as success. Fitzgerald: melancholic tone of the novel --> dreams often collapse when confronted with reality.
  • Self-destruction: Fitzgerald's battles with alcoholism and his gradual professional decline parallel Gatsby's revelation. Chapter 5: “If it wasn’t for the mist we could see your home across the bay”, said Gatsby. “You always have a green light that burns all night at the end of your dock”. → The green light at the end of Daisy's Pier symbolizes Gatsby's unattainable dream, as well as Fitzgerald's own struggles to reconcile his idealism with reality. It also reflects his longing for Zelda, whose mental health problems and material desires created an unbridgeable gap between them. Fitzgerald crafts a novel that critiques not only the societal flaws of the Jazz Age but also his own struggles with identity and fulfillment.
