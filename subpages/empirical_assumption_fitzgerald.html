“It seems to me that on one page I recognised a portion of an old diary of mine which mysteriously disappeared shortly after my marriage, and also scraps of letters which, though considerably edited, sound to me vaguely familiar. In fact, Mr. Fitzgerald . . . seems to believe that plagiarism begins at home” (Collected Writings 388).

The couple had been married for less than a year when he quipped in a January 1921 interview, “Indeed, I married the heroine of my stories. I would not be interested in any other woman” (qtd. in Milford 77).

And when an editor, impressed by his wife’s writing, wanted to publish her diaries around this same time, he refused to allow him to do so because the editor later claimed, Fitzgerald admitted he was too dependent on them for material for his own fiction to share them with anyone else (qtd. in Milford 71).

Highlights from Isabel Carrasco’s Article

Isabel Carrasco identifies key points in her article named “How F. Scott Fitzgerald, Author Of ‘The Great Gatsby,’ Plagiarized His Own Wife”:

  • F. Scott Fitzgerald is accused of plagiarizing his wife Zelda’s writings, particularly in The Beautiful and Damned.
  • Zelda’s personal writings, including diaries and letters, were used in Scott’s novels without credit.
  • Zelda criticized Scott's plagiarism, even reviewing his works to accuse him of stealing from her.
  • Tensions between them grew as Scott edited Zelda’s own manuscript, fearing it resembled his.
  • Their real-life struggles, including Zelda’s mental health, influenced Scott’s The Great Gatsby.

Key Points from J. Stolarek’s Article

“The Influence of Zelda Fitzgerald on F. Scott Fitzgerald’s Life and Literary Output” by J. Stolarek explores the intricate and often turbulent relationship between Zelda and F. Scott Fitzgerald and how their dynamic significantly impacted his life and works.

Zelda as F. Scott's Muse and Co-creator

  • Zelda inspired many of F. Scott Fitzgerald’s characters, particularly his portrayals of women in his novel Tender Is the Night.
  • Her letters were sometimes directly adapted or referenced in F. Scott’s writing, illustrating her influence on his creative process.

Mutual Creative Exchange

  • The relationship between F. Scott and Zelda was marked by collaboration, but also tension regarding intellectual ownership.
  • Zelda’s letters often inspired parts of F. Scott’s work, but her contributions were sometimes overshadowed by his public recognition.

Themes of Marital Struggle

The decline of their relationship mirrored the personal failure present in his novels. Zelda’s frustrations are highlighted in her own novel (Save Me the Waltz), which also drew from their shared experiences.

Impact of Zelda’s Mental Health

  • Zelda’s mental health struggles impacted F. Scott’s later works, such as Tender Is the Night, which reflects the emotional toll and complexities of their life together.
  • Her admittance into an institution for her mental health and their separation (she requested a divorce but he denied) are viewed as pivotal moments that shaped the darker tones of F. Scott’s later writing.

F. Scott’s Protective Nature Toward Zelda’s Legacy

  • Despite their conflicts, F. Scott was deeply protective of Zelda’s artistic talent and reputation, often defending her potential as a writer and painter.
  • However, while this dynamic highlights Zelda's influence on Scott's work, it is not always characterized as theft but rather as a complex collaboration in which Scott often takes the lead.
