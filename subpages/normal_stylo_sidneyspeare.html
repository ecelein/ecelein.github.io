Normal Stylo

The first analysis is conducted using the “Stylo” package for R, enabling an investigation of stylistic differences or similarities through stylometric analysis. The tool is leveraged for the quantitative nature of its analysis, whose results are based on calculations of the frequency of words and the examination of patterns. The results are then translated into visualizations, namely dendrograms, created for this analysis. The tree-like diagrams are particularly effective in identifying and organizing marker words for each author, allowing to successfully define writing styles, resulting in potential matches or clear distinctions.

To mitigate inherent limitations of the analytical tool, certain modifications were applied to the corpus. Firstly, since a small corpus might create faulty results, all tragedies by Shakespeare were added to expand it. This way, a standard for Shakespeare’s tragedies’ stylistic features was created, allowing for a more robust comparison and avoiding the misinterpretation of random patterns as significant results that can skew the outcome. Additionally, a distractor corpus of authors contemporary to Mary Sidney and William Shakespeare was added, following Juola’s principle stating that “It is therefore imperative to have a reasonable set of so-called distractor authors, authors who are either other reasonable suspects or at least similar”. This inclusion granted to establish a baseline for stylistic features typical of the period and limit confirmation bias, acting as a control mechanism.

The distractor authors corpus was initially set to three additional texts, in line with Juola’s indications which pinpointed an ideal number between 3 and 7, but was later, under suggestion, raised to 8 in order to add validity to the analysis. In fact, a higher corpus allows for more visually clear and significant clustering, lowering the chances of misinterpretation. Lastly, to avoid genre confusion and ensure continuity across stylistic conventions and vocabulary, only tragedies were taken into consideration.

One contains all works by Shakespeare, who was an innovator lexically and stylistically wise, indicating a distinct style. The results show overall consistency within his stylistic features, allowing confident attribution to Shakespeare of all his tragedies. The second branch features Mary Sidney’s tragedy and the rest of the distractor corpus. The two texts of interest (The tragedie of Antonie and Antony and Cleopatra) are, therefore, quite far on the graph, indicating a lack of common stylistic features. It must be taken into consideration that this specific analysis considers only basic textual information without its context, therefore losing information about potential borrowings of phrases or expressions.

Conclusions

Based on the findings from all the dendrograms, it appears that Shakespeare’s Antony and Cleopatra and Sidney’s The tragedie of Antonie diverge significantly in terms of style and syntax. The stylistic and structural differences between the tragedies offer explanations for their distinct linguistic patterns observed in the visualizations. Sidney's work adheres to classical conventions, employing monologues and observing Aristotelian unities, while Shakespeare's play features dialogue-heavy scenes and disregards these traditional constraints. The different portrayals of central characters also contributes: Sidney's Cleopatra embodies themes of power, self-determination, loyalty, and morality, while her Antony is presented more passively. In contrast, Shakespeare's Cleopatra is characterized by sexuality and emotional volatility, with Antony given a more complex representation. Despite sharing themes such as love, duty, stoicism vs. emotion, female agency, and moral reflection, the two works explore these concepts differently. The visualization captures not just differences in writing style, but also reflects deeper contrasts in dramatic approach, character conception, and thematic exploration between these two interpretations of the Antony and Cleopatra story. The stylometric analysis proves valid, as it correctly clusters all Shakespearian texts, however, it might not be the optimal tool to detect potential similarities between the two texts. In fact, it might miss similar nuances in character portrayal that require deeper contextual understanding, or idiomatic phrases that would be best understood in context. Additionally, words that appear infrequently but are used commonly in the two texts might be overlooked by the tool that prioritizes high-frequency terms. In conclusion, the tool does not suggest strong similarities between the two tragedies, although its limitations leave room for hypotheses about other similarities, which would require complementary qualitative analysis to be better appreciated.
