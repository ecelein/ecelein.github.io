ZETA ANALYSIS

The following analysis is performed to complement the assessments derived from the examination of the dendrograms, and further investigate potential stylistic matches and borrowings by Shakespeare from Sidney’s work. In fact, Craig’s Zeta offers lists of distinctive words, preferred and avoided by the two texts, that characterize the texts themselves. This investigation can better unveil textual relationships that cannot be appreciated using normal Stylo, which does not provide an overview of linguistic features, yet only stylistic clustering.

Primary set: The Tragedie of Antonie

Secondary set: Anthony and Cleopatra

Structure of the graph:

The y-axis ranks words based on their distinctiveness between the two sets, and the x-axis shows their relative preference in either author's works. Given the difference in corpus size between the two tragedies of about 10,000 words, the slice length was adjusted to 1000 to ensure multiple slices from both texts, increasing the statistical reliability and providing a more granular analysis.

Analysis

Based on immediate considerations, the lists present both differences and similarities. Many terms appear recurrently in both authors’ works, although under different forms: simple verbs such as “have” or “leave”, and words such as “never”, “up”, “upon”, which do not extensively corroborate the idea of stylistic influences. Other, more characteristic words can be observed, such as “poor”, “honor”, “queen”, which could also only be reflective of the shared themes of the two tragedies (the same characters' names also appear).

Sidney’s preferred list features terms of archaic spelling such as “haue”, “loue”, “nought”, “neuer”, absent in the avoided list (Shakespeare’s preferred words), which in turn contains “have”, “give”, “never”. This distinction could be related to chronological reasons, as Sidney’s version is 15 years older than Shakespeare’s, or to her classical upbringing and high education, suggesting a preference for drawing from traditions of elevated literature. A further explanation could have to do with a stylistic choice in the translation of Robert Garnier’s “Antoine”, adapting to the original’s lexicon.

The presence of theatrical jargon in Shakespeare’s preferred list (“exeunt”, “enter”, “scene”), not found in Sidney’s, can be justified by the nature of the former’s plays, which were meant to be played on stage, contrary to Sidney’s, who translated it as a closet drama (to be read rather than performed).

The preponderance of character names in Shakespeare’s preferred list rather than Sidney’s could be significative of Shakespeare’s use of dialogue, again for the sake of public representation, contrasting with the more prominent use of monologue in Sidney’s translation.

Words like “love”, “grief”, “woe”, and “fear” appear in both lists, suggesting that both authors emphasize emotional depth in their portrayal of characters and events. Also, both authors use elevated, poetic language, yet of different sorts, as seen in words like "faire", "sunne", and "deare" in Sidney's list, and "art", "speak", and "bear" in Shakespeare's.

Sidney’s vocabulary is more abundant in terms related to violence: “bloud”, “paine”, “armes”, “death” might indicate a more tragic, active twist. It is important to remember that Shakespeare, as a stylistic choice, steered away from the classical tradition and chose to represent battles and deaths on stage, which could translate into a lower need to write about related terms, decreasing their presence in the vocabulary.

Additionally, the presence of terms relating to specific settings in Shakespeare’s list (“Egypt”, “Alexandria”, “Pompey”, “palace”), absent or very general in Sidney (“realm”), could be connected to the Stratford-upon-Avon playwright’s disregard for the Aristotelian unit of place, respected by his female counterpart. The scenes could have been set in different contexts, reflected in the narration.

Conclusions

In conclusion, the stylistic differences highlighted by the normal Stylo analysis persist in Craig’s Zeta and seem to be corroborated by some distinctions in lexical preferences. Nevertheless, employing similar emotionally-ridden terms could account for Shakespeare’s decision to take inspiration from Sidney.

Limitations of the tool

One corpus being significantly larger, the statistical weight of its data dominates the analysis: high-frequency words might be incorrectly interpreted as stylistically significant simply because of the corpus’ size. Conversely, in smaller corpora, context-specific words appear more prominent because they represent a larger proportion of the limited word count.

Moreover, the topics of the two tragedies are the same, so many words appearing in the lists will unavoidably be related, without actually bringing evidence of influences. Those could be better detected by analyzing words in context.

Finally, as Craig’s Zeta reports single words without context, terms with double meanings could be misinterpreted.
