Contributions to the Field

This paper contributes significantly to studies on authorial influence, stylometry, and literary analysis to the extent that very modern computational methods shall be applied in an attempt to investigate the stylistic features that might reveal the affinity of famous literary authors regarding their authorship. Employing tools like Voyant, and R-Env will quantify linguistic patterns and stylistic features found in T.S. Eliot, Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot, William Shakespeare, Mary Sidney, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and Zelda Fitzgerald. In fact, such a novel use of a distractor corpora, argued for by Patrick Juola, will also facilitate the identification of subtle influences and probable cases of literary borrowing or even plagiarism.

The innovative nature of this project is in actually pairing with discussion topics that have rarely been held throughout history: Eliot and his wife, Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot, and the complex literary relationship between F. Scott Fitzgerald and his wife, Zelda Fitzgerald. More importantly, the investigation into the potential impact of Mary Sidney on the works of Shakespeare, in particular their shared themes and stylistic devices, calls for new perspectives on women's contribution in male-dominated contexts. This application of stylometry to the study of these dynamics has brought this subfield of authorship attribution to new frontiers and complicates the standard understanding of influence and collaboration.

Purpose of the Research

The leading purpose of the present research is an analysis of stylistic features and identification of authors of some celebrated literary figures by means of comparing their works with the use of the most current computational methods. By a series of carefully constructed pairings-T.S. Eliot and Vivienne Haigh-Wood Eliot, Shakespeare and Mary Sidney, and F. Scott Fitzgerald and Zelda Fitzgerald. The paper tries to trace influences and interactions that could have presumably influenced their various outputs. The study is empirical research based on assumptions supported by historical context, which may be of help in demystifying hitherto lesser-known relationships of these authors concerning spousal and female author influence within the literary circles.

The current research has filled in a number of gaps that were missing in the literary scholarship, particularly related to the area of gender dynamics in literature. New insights into the previously hardly discussed female contribution to male writers' creative work also offer new challenges to the traditional narratives about authorship and collaboration. Going further still, it reflects a more formal data-driven approach to authorship attribution beyond the traditional biographical or historical analytical approach toward one that is a more unvarnished quantitative examination of influence in literature. With that, this work will contribute significantly both to the knowledge of the field of literary scholarship and computational analysis since it is considered an extension of studies about how authors and the assessment of literary influence over time.
